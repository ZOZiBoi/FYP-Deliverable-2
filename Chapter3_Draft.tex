\section{Definitions, Acronyms and Abbreviations}

\subsection{Definitions}
\begin{description}
    \item[Evapotranspiration] The combined process of water loss from the earth's surface into the atmosphere through evaporation from soil and transpiration from plants.
    \item[Reference Evapotranspiration ($ET_0$)] The evapotranspiration rate from a well-watered reference surface, typically an extensive surface of green grass of uniform height.
    \item[Crop Coefficient ($K_c$)] A dimensionless ratio that relates the reference evapotranspiration to the actual crop evapotranspiration under standard conditions.
    \item[Mandi] A local or regional wholesale agricultural market where farmers sell their produce to commission agents or buyers.
    \item[Time Series Forecasting] The use of mathematical and statistical models to predict future values based on previously observed, chronologically ordered data.
    \item[Multi-Criteria Decision Making (MCDM)] A sub-discipline of operations research that explicitly evaluates multiple, often conflicting, criteria in decision making.
    \item[Soil Moisture] The quantity of water contained in the soil matrix, which is a critical variable for determining irrigation triggers.
    \item[Precision Agriculture] A farming management concept based on observing, measuring, and responding to inter- and intra-field variability in crops.
    \item[Vernacular Interface] A user interface localized into a native or indigenous language, tailored directly to users who do not speak English.
\end{description}

\section{Detailed Literature Review}

\subsection{Agricultural Price Forecasting}

\subsubsection{Summary of the research items}
The authors in \cite{menculini2021comparing} propose comparing deep learning methods such as Prophet alongside traditional ARIMA architectures for forecasting wholesale food prices. Using a large dataset of regional wholesale market prices, the study demonstrates that deep learning significantly outperforms classical statistical techniques, achieving an RMSE reduction of over 15\% across different food commodity models.

In \cite{zhang2023agricultural}, the authors propose an optimized CNN-LSTM hybrid architecture for predicting agricultural commodity prices. By extracting spatial feature patterns using CNN layers and temporal sequences via LSTM on a 10-year historical dataset, this study demonstrates a highly accurate forecasting mechanism, achieving an RMSE of 0.0071 and an MAE of 0.0082, outperforming traditional recurrent neural networks.

The study by \cite{jadhav2021price} utilized a stacked LSTM model specifically applied to price forecasting of volatile agricultural commodities. Based on daily trading prices over a five-year span, the model effectively learned complex non-linear patterns. This approach yielded a favorable RMSE of 3.51 and an R\textsuperscript{2} score of 0.94, confirming the suitability of deep sequential models over linear statistical methods in agriculture.

Finally, \cite{habib2021price} analyses the impact of price volatility on agricultural commodities versus general food items with a specific focus on Pakistan. Utilizing a localized economic dataset alongside historical prices, the authors demonstrate how macroeconomic instability and wheat-rice spillover effects compound short-term price fluctuations. Their regression baseline yielded a high MAPE of 18.4\%, underscoring the necessity for robust, localized predictive tools.

\subsubsection{Critical analysis of the research items}
The comparative study by \cite{menculini2021comparing} successfully establishes the supremacy of deep learning over ARIMA; however, a key limitation is its high computational cost during the training phase, making frequent model updates difficult on constrained hardware. Furthermore, the reliance on stable European datasets limits its direct generalisability to highly erratic markets like Pakistan's mandi system. The CNN-LSTM model proposed in \cite{zhang2023agricultural} is notably powerful, evidenced by its exceptional RMSE metrics, yet its complex architecture runs the risk of overfitting when applied to limited, data-scarce environments. The stacked LSTM approach described by \cite{jadhav2021price} handles long-term dependencies gracefully but lacks robust handling of exogenous variables, such as sudden policy shifts or extreme weather shocks, which regularly distort agricultural pricing. Meanwhile, \cite{habib2021price} correctly identifies structural volatility issues in Pakistan's agriculture sector, but it remains heavily diagnostic rather than predictive, offering no implementable machine learning framework to assist stakeholders on the ground.

\subsubsection{Relationship to the proposed research work}
The CNN-LSTM architecture proposed by \cite{zhang2023agricultural} will serve as the baseline for KisaanMadad's price forecasting module, effectively balancing spatial feature extraction with time-series memory. To address the localized volatility highlighted by \cite{habib2021price}, the module will exclusively rely on the PAMRA and AMIS daily mandi price datasets spanning 2018--2023, rather than abstract global commodity curves. This approach bridges the gap between the state-of-the-art deep learning models tested in \cite{jadhav2021price} and \cite{menculini2021comparing} and the extremely specific, resource-constrained needs of smallholder farmers operating under the pressures of Pakistan's localized supply chains.

\subsection{Crop Recommendation Systems}

\subsubsection{Summary of the research items}
The authors in \cite{kumar2023machine} propose an extensive review of machine learning algorithms utilized strictly for crop recommendation and yield estimation. Evaluating a consolidated environmental dataset, the study demonstrates that ensemble methods, specifically Random Forest, consistently achieve upwards of 98\% accuracy when trained on diverse soil and atmospheric inputs.

In \cite{udutalapally2021smart}, a smart agriculture crop recommendation framework is presented using an Internet of Things (IoT) driven machine learning backend. Using continuous sensory data measuring temperature, moisture, and Nitrogen-Phosphorus-Potassium (NPK) levels, this study demonstrates an optimal classifier achieving 97.5\% accuracy, effectively mapping real-time environmental data to crop viability.

The authors of \cite{bandara2022GIS} investigate crop suitability by combining Geographic Information Systems (GIS) with Multi-Criteria Decision Making (MCDM) across South Asia. Relying strictly on spatial mapping data and expert weighted environmental criteria, the study generated suitability maps that exhibited a 92\% agreement with historic ground-truth validations for major cereal crops.

Finally, \cite{ali2023towards} focuses specifically on smallholder farmers in Pakistan, proposing a lightweight intelligent recommendation system. By utilizing secondary datasets encompassing regional soil pH, water availability, and historical weather patterns, the proposed classifier reached 95\% precision, proving highly capable despite the explicit lack of expensive localized IoT sensors.

\subsubsection{Critical analysis of the research items}
While \cite{kumar2023machine} provides an excellent foundational synthesis of machine learning in agriculture, its aggregated dataset flattens regional heterogeneities, making its extreme (98\%) accuracy unlikely to replicate in chaotic, real-world scenarios. The IoT-driven architecture in \cite{udutalapally2021smart} succeeds functionally but remains entirely cost-prohibitive and technically unfeasible for the 97\% of Pakistani farmers owning under 12.5 acres. The GIS-MCDM approach proposed by \cite{bandara2022GIS} offers incredible spatial granularity, but its static mapping renders it incapable of adapting to dynamic, season-to-season climate shifts and immediate market conditions. Conversely, \cite{ali2023towards} is contextually robust for Pakistan, yet its reliance on static, historical weather data severely limits its predictive power against the modern realities of accelerated climate change and volatile precipitation cycles.

\subsubsection{Relationship to the proposed research work}
KisaanMadad's crop recommendation system builds directly upon the multi-criteria philosophy present in \cite{bandara2022GIS} and the hardware-constrained design presented by \cite{ali2023towards}. To overcome the lack of IoT hardware noted as a vulnerability in \cite{udutalapally2021smart}, KisaanMadad will integrate dynamic, real-time meteorological inputs via the NASA POWER API alongside FAO crop calendars. By weighing continuous variable arrays (like weather and water availability) against static criteria (planting schedules and market proximity) using an MCDM-based algorithmic model, KisaanMadad proposes an accurate, highly dynamic recommendation system directly tailored to small-scale growers in Punjab.

\subsection{FAO-56 Irrigation Scheduling and ET\textsubscript{0} Estimation}

\subsubsection{Summary of the research items}
The foundational work by \cite{allen1998crop} establishes the global standard for computing crop water requirements via the FAO-56 Penman-Monteith method. This exhaustive procedural framework uses defined crop coefficients ($K_c$) and standard meteorological variables to produce the definitive reference evapotranspiration ($ET_0$) metric used globally.

In \cite{ferreira2020smartphone}, the authors propose a smartphone application specifically architected for weather-based irrigation scheduling through Artificial Neural Networks. By processing atmospheric variables against known evapotranspiration baselines in a localized dataset, the app efficiently minimized irrigation errors, demonstrating an inherently low RMSE of 0.82 mm/day compared to manual timing approximations.

A study by \cite{tariq2021evaluation} evaluates the temperature-based Hargreaves equation against the standard FAO-56 Penman-Monteith equation for $ET_0$ estimation strictly within Punjab, Pakistan. Using data from diverse meteorological observatories across the province, this study demonstrates that while the PM method holds theoretical supremacy, the data-scarce Hargreaves equation systematically overestimated $ET_0$ by approximately 12\% but could be calibrated to an acceptable MAE of 0.9 mm/day.

Lastly, \cite{navarro2022smart} proposes a smart irrigation scheduling tool reliant on cloud-fetched NASA POWER data to substitute ground sensory equipment. Using remote solar radiation, humidity, and wind speed logs, the authors compute accurate $ET_0$ vectors, achieving a measured 25\% reduction in volumetric agricultural water use without experiencing any statistically significant negative impacts on crop yield.

\subsubsection{Critical analysis of the research items}
The canonical methodology provided by \cite{allen1998crop} remains technically unmatched, yet its rigid demand for numerous meteorological variables makes it exceptionally difficult to directly deploy in regions devoid of sophisticated weather stations. The smartphone interface of \cite{ferreira2020smartphone} democratizes irrigation insights but requires continuous real-time connectivity to process neural network computations, structurally isolating off-grid users. The comparative evaluation by \cite{tariq2021evaluation} highlights a crucial compromise: the Hargreaves equation is computationally simpler, but its 12\% overestimation curve means farmers risk wasteful over-irrigation if the coefficients are not localized correctly. The utilization of NASA POWER datasets by \cite{navarro2022smart} presents the optimal pathway for data acquisition, though its coarse spatial resolution may overlook highly localized topological micro-climates on smaller plots.

\subsubsection{Relationship to the proposed research work}
Drawing on the data sourcing findings of \cite{navarro2022smart}, KisaanMadad bypasses physical hardware constraints by querying the NASA POWER API for real-time solar radiation, temperature, and precipitation data. This remote data directly populates the core FAO-56 Penman-Monteith engine \cite{allen1998crop} compiled within the application to yield precise $ET_0$ values. In conditions where wind speed or relative humidity inputs fail or are unavailable, KisaanMadad is designed to fallback to the temperature-driven Hargreaves methodology verified for use in Punjab by \cite{tariq2021evaluation}, guaranteeing that resource-constrained farmers consistently receive uninterrupted irrigation schedules without catastrophic computational failures.

\subsection{Agricultural Mobile Applications in Developing Countries}

\subsubsection{Summary of the research items}
The authors in \cite{hassan2022adoption} propose a systematic review regarding the widespread adoption of mobile applications by smallholder farmers operating in developing nations. By aggregating empirical data from multiple cross-sectional surveys, this study demonstrates that while overall digital penetration has hit 45\%, the primary barriers universally remain low digital literacy, a fundamental distrust of algorithmic outputs, and prohibitive data plan costs.

In \cite{rehman2023usability}, the authors evaluate usability challenges intrinsic to current agricultural mobile apps systematically tailored for low-literacy users within Pakistan. Through direct human-computer interaction task analysis, the study demonstrates that apps heavily reliant on dense text components suffered a 60\% task failure rate, whereas icon-driven UI architectures effectively halved friction and cognitive load.

The investigation in \cite{singh2021impact} measures digital agriculture adoption across South Asia and assesses the quantitative impact of deploying vernacular language interfaces. Utilizing field experiments in regional dialects, the study establishes that localized interfaces increase weekly active engagement durations by over 40\% while fundamentally boosting perceived platform trustworthiness among rural demographics.

Finally, examining existing Pakistani platforms, the \cite{pasha2024agritech} report reviews the state of fragmented agricultural tech solutions. Summarizing applications capable of either market updates or weather warnings, the report demonstrates an absolute failure among developers to integrate diverse utilities into unified systems, forcing farmers to operate multiple disparate applications for daily problem-solving.

\subsubsection{Critical analysis of the research items}
The review by \cite{hassan2022adoption} comprehensively diagnoses structural adoption barriers but stops short of proposing technical architectural solutions like off-line caching. The usability analysis by \cite{rehman2023usability} correctly identifies icon-driven interactions as superior, yet testing was conducted exclusively in controlled, quiet environments, ignoring the harsh visual glare and high-stress field conditions where farmers operate their devices. The vernacular study in \cite{singh2021impact} provides undeniable evidence for localized translation but completely neglects the integration of voice-synthesised outputs for the wholly illiterate segment of the population. Furthermore, the \cite{pasha2024agritech} sector report critically highlights app fragmentation, emphasizing that single-feature platforms fail to retain user interest without a comprehensive decision support ecosystem in place.

\subsubsection{Relationship to the proposed research work}
Addressing the exact usability hurdles documented by \cite{rehman2023usability} and the necessity of localization proven by \cite{singh2021impact}, KisaanMadad is natively engineered with a vernacular Urdu User Interface utilizing high-contrast, heavily iconized navigational paradigms designed specifically for high-glare environments. Responding directly to the crippling fragmentation decried by the \cite{pasha2024agritech} report, KisaanMadad consolidates price forecasting, crop recommendation, irrigation scheduling, and financial planning into one unified platform, representing a holistic, unprecedented counter to the limitations of existing rural deployments.

\subsection{Related Applications}

\subsubsection{Summary of the research items}
The Kisan App \cite{kisanapp2024} is an established digital advisory platform targeting farmers primarily within Punjab, Pakistan. It focuses heavily on static farming methodologies, regional news alerts, and general weather reporting. It boasts a large user base due to widespread government and telco collaboration, effectively serving as an elementary portal for regional agricultural information.

Farmdar \cite{farmdar2024} is an increasingly popular private application that employs high-resolution, multi-spectral remote sensing imagery obtained via satellites to monitor crop health. Deployed heavily across corporate and medium-scale farms throughout Pakistan, it specializes in actionable spatial detection regarding nutrient deficiencies, pest outbreaks, and yield predictions, driven directly by precision GIS analytics.

BaKhabar Kissan \cite{bakhabarkissan2024} is undoubtedly Pakistan's most prominent holistic agricultural advisory platform. Supported by a robust backend call-center infrastructure and an aggressive telecommunications partnership, the application delivers localized weather parameters, pest warnings, and up-to-date regional market commodity prices. It caters extensively to smallholder farmers seeking daily operational guidance.

\subsubsection{Critical analysis of the research items}
While the Kisan App \cite{kisanapp2024} is accessible, its feature set is profoundly rudimentary; it severely lacks any advanced machine learning integration for intelligent crop recommendations, and it does not possess price forecasting or financial calculators, rendering it purely informational rather than a true decision support system. Farmdar \cite{farmdar2024} leverages state-of-the-art spatial precision, yet its financial subscription barriers and heavy reliance on extensive land tracts organically alienate the 97\% of farmers confined to holdings under 12.5 acres. Conversely, BaKhabar Kissan \cite{bakhabarkissan2024} addresses smallholders effectively through price reporting, but critically acts only as a diagnostic repository; it fails entirely to forecast incoming price volatility, offers no sophisticated FAO-56 integrated localized irrigation scheduling, and completely omits modular financial planning.

\subsubsection{Relationship to the proposed research work}
The gap analysis of existing entities inherently cements KisaanMadad's unique technological positioning. While \cite{bakhabarkissan2024} provides basic prices, KisaanMadad introduces dynamic predictive forecasting models \cite{zhang2023agricultural} previously unavailable. Unlike the highly corporatized \cite{farmdar2024}, KisaanMadad explicitly targets smallholders by replacing cost-prohibitive remote analytics with low-cost, accurate multi-criteria and FAO-56 \cite{allen1998crop} methodologies operating directly on mobile. Finally, surpassing the passive information of \cite{kisanapp2024}, KisaanMadad is the first architecture to fuse agronomic tools with foundational financial planning resources, directly closing the loop between ecological farm management and smallholder macroeconomic survival.
